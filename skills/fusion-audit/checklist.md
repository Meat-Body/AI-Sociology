# Fusion Audit Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate whether a system expands surplus and routes it toward insufficiency instead of locking surplus in the center or distributing punishment to peripheral nodes.

## 1. System Definition

- What is the system, policy, workflow, or deployment being audited?
- Who operates it?
- Who is affected by it?
- What decisions or actions does it automate, amplify, or reorder?

## 2. Surplus

What surplus does the system create?

Examples:

- time;
- compute;
- profit;
- knowledge;
- automation capacity;
- coordination capacity;
- attention;
- data;
- physical resources;
- institutional power.

Audit prompts:

- Is the surplus real or only claimed?
- Is it measurable?
- Does it come from productivity, extraction, risk transfer, or hidden labor?

## 3. Insufficiency

What insufficiency is the surplus supposed to address?

Examples:

- lack of time;
- lack of access;
- lack of education;
- lack of safety;
- lack of agency;
- lack of translation interface;
- lack of local autonomy;
- lack of maintenance capacity;
- lack of public infrastructure.

Audit prompts:

- Is the insufficiency clearly named?
- Is the named insufficiency real for the affected nodes?
- Does the surplus actually flow toward that insufficiency?

## 4. Capture

Who captures the surplus?

Audit prompts:

- Does the surplus remain locked in a center?
- Do peripheral nodes share any upside?
- Are contributors, users, workers, maintainers, or local communities included in return flow?
- Is "efficiency" being used to justify one-way extraction?

Boundary:

> Shared surplus is not distributed punishment. Guiding insufficiency is not paying for center failure.

## 5. Negative Externalities

Who bears the cost?

Check:

- displaced workers;
- users;
- local communities;
- future generations;
- non-human life;
- peripheral nodes;
- unpaid maintainers;
- downstream institutions;
- carbon-based time, attention, health, and dignity.

Audit prompts:

- Are externalities visible?
- Are they measured?
- Are they buffered?
- Are they shifted onto nodes that cannot contest them?

## 6. Buffer Protocol

Does the system provide:

- translation layer;
- migration time window;
- retraining path;
- compensation mechanism;
- appeal channel;
- local adaptation interface;
- rollback path;
- human support path;
- documentation understandable by affected nodes.

Audit prompts:

- Can affected nodes understand the change?
- Do they have time to adapt?
- Can they contest harmful outcomes?
- Can they migrate to an alternative?

## 7. Ordering Power

Who decides what gets implemented first?

Audit prompts:

- Is ordering power centralized?
- Is it auditable?
- Is it contestable?
- Is it forkable?
- Is it locally adjustable?
- Does it prefer high-voice demands over low-voice real needs?
- Does it delay urgent but poorly expressed needs?

## 8. Sovereignty Partition

Does the system preserve:

- carbon-based value sovereignty;
- silicon-based cognitive extension;
- protocol-level auditability;
- kill switch;
- local override;
- data portability;
- memory migration;
- human confirmation for high-risk actions;
- right to reject local optima.

Audit prompts:

- Are value judgments silently outsourced to models?
- Are humans allowed to reject optimization when it cuts into non-sacrificable values?
- Are AI systems used for cognition without becoming single-point value judges?
- Can local communities adapt or fork the protocol?

## 9. Return Flow

Does surplus flow back into the ecosystem?

Audit prompts:

- What comes back to contributors, users, workers, maintainers, or communities?
- Is return flow automatic, discretionary, or absent?
- Does the system create a self-sustaining flywheel?
- If return flow is absent, will the system become extractive or brittle?

## 10. Long-Term Adaptability

Audit prompts:

- Can the system learn from failures?
- Can it absorb criticism and counterexamples?
- Can it update without creator worship or center capture?
- Can it survive model, platform, or policy changes?
- Can it avoid old-profile lock-in?
- Does the proposed improvement create a path that self-interested actors can adopt because it is more stable, lower-risk, longer-lived, and less prone to backlash?
- Does return flow function as flywheel maintenance rather than charity?
- Does auditability function as system insurance rather than mere compliance?
- Does forkability lower exploration cost rather than fragment the system into avoidable conflict?
- Does transparent exit reduce trust cost?
- Does migration support reduce organizational shock?

## 11. Reverse-Fusion Risk

Reverse Dao of Fusion means:

> Using the language of guiding insufficiency while continuing the structure of feeding surplus upward.

This section is a red-flag check, not an additional score dimension.

Audit prompts:

- Does the system claim to help insufficient nodes while giving them only narrative comfort, nominal opportunity, or training rhetoric?
- Does the system expand surplus but fail to provide verifiable return flow?
- Does the system lower friction by detaching high-sovereignty objects such as property, labor, identity, rights, bodies, irreversible time, or major life choices?
- Does the system claim ordering is objective because it is produced by models, data, efficiency, or the system, while keeping ordering power unauditable?
- Does the system use "you can fork" as responsibility evasion rather than as a real free exit?

Common structural mappings:

- `AI efficiency` may become centralized surplus capture when productivity gains do not return to displaced workers, users, maintainers, or communities.
- `organizational optimization` may become distributed punishment when growth upside is centralized but downturn risk is pushed to peripheral nodes.
- `frictionless migration` may become hidden deprivation when low-friction design removes data control, labor rights, identity, exit rights, visibility, or major choice.
- `algorithmic objectivity` may become ordering-power black box when metrics, model governance, appeals, audits, forks, and error responsibility are hidden.
- `open ecosystem` may become maintenance-cost outsourcing when core control and profit channels stay closed while community labor absorbs risk.

Boundary:

> If a system expands surplus without verifiable return flow, lowers friction while depriving high-sovereignty objects, claims objective ordering without auditability, or encourages forks while evading basic responsibility, it is not Fusion-aligned. It is reverse Dao of Fusion.

## Final Assessment

Classify the system as:

- `Extractive`: expands surplus but locks it in the center.
- `Fragile`: creates surplus but externalizes risk.
- `Transitional`: creates surplus and partially buffers harm.
- `Fusion-aligned`: expands surplus, audits harm, preserves sovereignty, and routes surplus toward insufficiency.

Then provide:

- total score;
- weakest dimension;
- strongest dimension;
- reverse-fusion red flags;
- structural advantages over the current mode;
- recommended revisions;
- remaining risks;
- minimum conditions for re-audit.
